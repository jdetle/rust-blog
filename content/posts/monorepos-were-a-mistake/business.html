Repository fragmentation represents a compounding operational cost that most engineering organizations underestimate. When teams maintain isolated codebases with duplicated infrastructure — identical build pipelines, overlapping dependency management, redundant deployment configurations — the maintenance overhead scales superlinearly with the number of repositories.

The risk profile is straightforward: a critical vulnerability discovered in shared infrastructure must be patched across every affected repository. In a fragmented environment, this process is manual, inconsistent, and slow. The exposure window between discovery and full remediation grows with each additional repo, directly increasing the organization's security and compliance risk.

From a resource allocation perspective, senior engineers spend a disproportionate amount of their time on cross-repository coordination rather than value-creating work. This represents a misallocation of your highest-cost talent. Consolidating into multi-package repositories reduces this coordination tax and redirects engineering capacity toward product differentiation.

The competitive implication is clear: organizations that solve their repository fragmentation problem ship faster, patch vulnerabilities sooner, and deploy engineering resources more efficiently. Google and Meta adopted this model not as a technical preference but as a strategic capability — the ability to make atomic changes across the entire codebase is a force multiplier for large engineering organizations.

The recommendation is practical: rebrand the initiative from "monorepo migration" to "multi-package repository consolidation." The former triggers resistance from teams protective of their autonomy. The latter communicates the actual value proposition — shared infrastructure, preserved ownership boundaries, and reduced operational overhead.
